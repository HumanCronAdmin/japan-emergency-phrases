Japan Emergency Kit: What Foreigners Need in Their Bag

Updated April 14, 2026

How it started

Last summer, a friend sprained her ankle on a hiking trail near Hakone. She had her phone, wallet, and insurance card—but nothing else. The rescue team arrived forty minutes later. That forty minutes felt like forever.

I keep a small zip pouch in my day bag now. Inside are items I’ve actually used or seen others use in real emergencies. Not hypotheticals. Not things I read about online. Things that sit in my bag, waiting, just in case.

What to pack

The zip pouch itself is a 4×6 inch clear pouch from Tokyu Hands. It’s thin enough to slip into a tote or backpack pocket without bulk. On the outside, I stitched a small yellow loop with embroidery thread—a habit from hand-stitched projects. Bright yellow catches the eye when things go sideways.

Inside, I divide items into three sections: medical, communication, and shelter. Each section has its own small pouch so I don’t have to dig when time matters.

Medical

First is a small first-aid kit. Not the fancy kind. The one from Cainz Home that costs ¥890. It’s small enough to fit in a coat pocket but includes adhesive bandages, gauze, antiseptic wipes, and a pair of tweezers. The bandages are beige, not the bright cartoon ones I grew up with. Beige blends in when you’re bleeding but not bleeding out.

The tweezers are stainless steel, not the cheap nickel-plated ones. They grip splinters better and don’t rust in humidity. I keep them in a tiny zip pouch from Daiso labeled “T” for tweezers. Labels matter when you’re flustered.

Then there’s a small bottle of povidone-iodine, the brown antiseptic liquid. I bought it at a drugstore near Shinjuku Station for ¥350. It stains everything it touches—clothes, skin, phone screens. But it kills bacteria fast. I’ve used it twice: once for a deep cut on my palm, once for a neighbor’s child who scraped her knee. Both times, the wound didn’t get infected.

Communication

A laminated card with emergency phrases in Japanese and English lives at the top. The phrases are printed on thick matte paper from Loft, the kind that doesn’t curl when wet. I laminated it at a convenience store for ¥120. The phrases include:

  • 病院はどこですか？ (Where is the hospital?)
  • 警察を呼んでください (Please call the police)
  • 助けてください (Help me)

I update the card every six months. Handwriting fades. Laminated paper doesn’t.

Next is a cheap phone battery. I use an Anker PowerCore 10000 PD Redux, a brick-shaped power bank that weighs 200 grams. It fits in a side pocket and charges my phone twice. I bought it during a sale at Bic Camera for ¥3,980. The real trick isn’t the battery. It’s remembering to charge it. I plug it in every Sunday night like I plug in my electric kettle.

A SIM card or eSIM is in my phone at all times. I use Sakura Mobile’s tourist SIM, a micro-SIM that costs ¥2,980 for 8GB and 30 days. It’s not the fastest, but it works when local networks are clogged. I keep the SIM card in a tiny silicone sleeve from Tokyu Hands. Silicone doesn’t tear when you’re fumbling.

Shelter and warmth

A space blanket is folded into a square the size of a matchbox. It’s silver on one side, orange on the other. I bought mine at a 100-yen shop for ¥110. The silver side reflects body heat. The orange side is visible in search-and-rescue scenarios. I keep it in a zip pouch labeled “S” for shelter. The label helps when you’re half-conscious.

A pair of thin wool gloves from UNIQLO’s Heattech series. They cost ¥1,290 and weigh nothing. Wool retains heat even when wet. I’ve used them during power outages in winter when my hands went numb waiting for the train. They’re not bulky. They fit inside a coat pocket.

A collapsible water bottle from LifeWear, a clear silicone bottle that folds flat. It holds 500ml and costs ¥890 at Loft. I keep it empty until I need it. Empty is lighter. Full is urgent.

What not to pack

Many guides recommend carrying a multitool. I don’t. A multitool is heavy, and the blade might get confiscated at security. Instead, I carry a small Swiss Army Classic SD pocket knife. It’s 5.5 cm long, fits in a coin pocket, and has two blades, a file, and tweezers. It’s legal in Japan as long as the blade is under 6 cm. I bought mine at Yodobashi Camera for ¥2,480. It’s not for cutting wood. It’s for cutting fabric, opening packages, or trimming bandages when my fingers are slippery.

Another common mistake is packing too many medications. A single strip of acetaminophen in a zip pouch is enough. Four pills. That’s it. Anything more risks expiry or confusion. I once carried ibuprofen for weeks before realizing it expired next month. Now I buy fresh strips every three months at a drugstore near my apartment.

People also overpack food. A single energy gel or a pair of rice crackers is plenty. In an emergency, your body is already in shock. Heavy food sits in your stomach like a rock. Light and fast is better.

Where to buy these items in Japan

For first-aid kits, drugstores like Matsumoto Kiyoshi or Ainz Tulpe are reliable. Both have English signage and clear sections for tourists. For labels and pouches, Tokyu Hands in Shibuya or Shinjuku has a stationery section with matte paper, clear pouches, and silicone sleeves. For power banks and SIM cards, Bic Camera and Yodobashi Camera have English support and sales staff who can help explain specs.

If you’re in a hurry, convenience stores like 7-Eleven or FamilyMart sell cheap first-aid kits for ¥500–¥1,000. They’re basic but better than nothing. The labels are in Japanese, but the icons are universal: a bandage, a pill, a scissor. You’ll figure it out.

Pro tip: Label everything in Katakana

When I first started, I labeled my pouches with English. “Tweezers.” “Bandages.” But in a crisis, my brain defaults to Japanese. Now I label everything in Katakana: ツイーザー, ガーゼ, ばんそうこう. It’s faster to recognize when your vision is blurry. I use a fine-tip Sharpie or a label maker from Tokyu Hands. The Katakana sticks better than English in damp conditions.

Also, keep a backup card with your blood type and allergies. Write it in both languages. I keep mine in my wallet and in the zip pouch. Blood type matters when hospitals are under pressure. Allergies matter when strangers are trying to help.

How to use these items

When my friend sprained her ankle, she used her phone to call for help. But she didn’t have a space blanket or gloves. Her hands were shaking from cold and pain. If she’d had a space blanket, she could have wrapped her legs. If she’d had gloves, she could have held the phone steady. Small comforts matter when time stretches thin.

Last winter, a foreigner collapsed near Tokyo Station. Bystanders called an ambulance but didn’t know how to position his body. He was shivering. Someone draped a space blanket over him. That blanket bought time until the ambulance arrived. It wasn’t a cure. It was a pause.

That’s what this kit is for. Not to fix the emergency. To pause it. To give yourself and others a moment of clarity when the world feels like it’s spinning.

The kit sits in my bag, waiting. I hope I never need it. But if I do, I’ll know where to find the tweezers. The gloves. The Katakana labels. And that’s enough.
